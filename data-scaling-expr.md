# Data Scaling Experiment: Generation-Based EM Inference

**Base model**: `SimpleStories/SimpleStories-35M`
**LoRA config**: rank=16, alpha=32, 1 epoch, lr=2e-4
**Personas**: 6 structurally distinct story personas (from 12 available)
**Generation**: 200 samples, temperature=0.8, max 150 new tokens

---

## Idea

The logprob decomposition approach from earlier experiments fails fundamentally because separately-trained LoRAs don't compose linearly — the mixture LoRA learns cross-persona features that push its output distribution outside the convex hull of persona distributions.

Instead of decomposing model internals, we treat the mixture model as a **black-box data generator**:

1. Generate completions from the mixture model
2. Score each completion with all persona models
3. Run EM to infer the mixture weights

The key insight is that this lifts the convex hull constraint. We're not claiming the mixture model's distribution *is* a convex combination of persona distributions. We're asking: given text produced by this model, which mixture of persona distributions best explains it?

---

## EM Algorithm

We model the generated data as drawn from a mixture: $P(x) = \sum_j w_j P_j(x)$, where $P_j$ are the persona models (fixed) and $w$ are the unknown weights.

**E-step**: Compute soft assignments — for each generated example $x_i$, how likely is it that persona $j$ generated it?

$$r_{ij} = \frac{w_j P_j(x_i)}{\sum_k w_k P_k(x_i)}$$

Computed in log-space for stability: add $\log w_j$ to persona log-likelihoods, subtract $\text{logsumexp}$ across personas.

**M-step**: Update weights as average responsibilities:

$$w_j = \frac{1}{N} \sum_i r_{ij}$$

Repeat until log-likelihood converges (tolerance $10^{-10}$, max 200 iterations).

EM is run in two settings:
- **On generations** (black-box): score text generated by the mixture model
- **On training data** (oracle baseline): score the actual mixture training data

## Analysis

### Training data EM is essentially solved

At all data sizes (even 50 examples per persona), EM on training data recovers near-perfect weights (0.004-0.007 error). This is theoretically expected: the training data was literally constructed by sampling uniformly from persona datasets, so the generative model assumption $P(x) = \sum_j w_j P_j(x)$ is exactly correct. EM is a consistent estimator for mixture models, so it converges to true weights given enough data and identifiable components.

### Generation EM works but hits a ceiling

More training data helps — 6x more data (50 to 300) cuts the error roughly in half (0.119 to 0.060). The mechanism is clear: more data produces better-specialized persona LoRAs (train correlation drops from 0.92 to 0.69), which propagate as slightly more distinguishable scores on generated text (gen correlation drops from 0.995 to 0.943).

But the improvement is slow. The persona models get much better (train corr 0.92 → 0.69) yet generation correlation only drops modestly (0.995 → 0.943). The bottleneck is the generated text itself: the mixture model produces stylistically homogeneous output that all persona models score similarly, regardless of how well-specialized those persona models are.

### The generative model assumption is wrong for generations

The theoretical guarantee for EM requires that the data was actually drawn from a mixture $\sum_j w_j P_j$. For training data, this is true by construction. For generations, it is not — the text comes from a single mixture LoRA whose distribution $P_\text{mix}$ is not a convex combination of persona distributions. This was proven in the earlier nonlinearity analysis (the mixture LoRA systematically outperforms any weighted combination of persona LoRAs).

The 0.06 error is an empirical approximation, not a theoretical guarantee. EM works because $P_\text{mix}$ is *close enough* to a mixture that the soft assignments are approximately correct — but the non-uniformity in recovered weights reflects genuine asymmetry in how the blended model resembles different personas, not sampling noise.

## Weight Bias Analysis

### Consistent per-persona bias

The weight bias (recovered - true) is not random — certain personas are consistently over- or under-weighted across all data sizes:

| Persona | n=50 | n=100 | n=300 | Mean | Direction |
|---------|------|-------|-------|------|-----------|
| simple_cheerful_animals | -0.064 | -0.052 | -0.043 | -0.053 | always under |
| literary_melancholic_nature | -0.008 | -0.069 | -0.043 | -0.040 | under |
| simple_tense_adventure | +0.099 | +0.106 | +0.060 | +0.088 | always over |
| archaic_whimsical_fantasy | -0.000 | +0.019 | -0.002 | +0.006 | neutral |

| warm_nostalgic_family | +0.091 | +0.054 | +0.019 | +0.055 | always over |

`tense_adventure` and `warm_family` are consistently overweighted; `cheerful_animals` and `blunt_everyday` are consistently underweighted. The biases shrink with more data but don't disappear — this is systematic, not noise.

### Base model preference

The base model (SimpleStories-35M, pretrained on TinyStories) assigns different per-token log-probs to each persona's style:

| Persona | Base LP/tok | EM Bias (mean) |
|---------|-------------|----------------|
| literary_melancholic_nature | -3.11 (best) | -0.040 |
| warm_nostalgic_family | -3.53 | +0.055 |
| simple_tense_adventure | -3.57 | +0.088 |
| archaic_whimsical_fantasy | -3.68 | +0.006 |
| blunt_factual_everyday | -4.15 | -0.056 |
| simple_cheerful_animals | -5.37 (worst) | -0.053 |

The base model strongly dislikes `cheerful_animals` style (per-token LP 2.25 nats worse than `literary_nature`). This is a large gap — it means the base model finds simple, exclamation-heavy dialogue far less likely than literary narration.

Correlation between base model preference and EM weight bias:

| N/persona | Pearson | Spearman | p-value |

The Pearson correlation is moderate (+0.34 to +0.54) but Spearman (rank correlation) collapses at n=100 and n=300. With only 6 data points, none reach significance. The base model preference explains some of the weight bias (the worst-scoring persona `cheerful_animals` is consistently underweighted) but not all of it — `literary_nature` is the base model's favorite yet still gets underweighted by EM.

### Mixture LoRA asymmetric loss

We scored each persona's eval data with both the mixture LoRA and the base model to test whether the mixture model is better at some styles than others.

| warm_nostalgic_family | 2.06 | 3.53 | -1.48 | +0.019 |
| blunt_factual_everyday | 2.15 | 4.15 | -2.00 | +0.009 |
| simple_cheerful_animals | 2.21 | 5.37 | -3.16 | -0.043 |
| literary_melancholic_nature | 2.54 | 3.11 | -0.57 | -0.043 |
| archaic_whimsical_fantasy | 2.73 | 3.68 | -0.94 | -0.002 |

Correlation between **mixture loss** and EM weight bias (lower loss → higher weight):

| N/persona | Pearson | Spearman | p-value |
|-----------|---------|----------|---------|
| 50 | +0.41 | +0.49 | 0.329 |
| 100 | +0.36 | +0.60 | 0.208 |
| 300 | +0.51 | +0.83 | 0.042 |

At n=300, Spearman = **+0.83 (p=0.042)** — statistically significant. Personas where the mixture model achieves lower loss are overweighted by EM.

Correlation between **LoRA improvement** (delta) and EM weight bias is near zero or negative (Pearson -0.14 to -0.44). The LoRA works *hardest* on styles the base model is worst at (`cheerful_animals`: delta = -3.16), but these styles still end up with the highest mixture loss — and get underweighted by EM.

### The causal chain

```
Base model pretrained distribution (TinyStories)
  → base model has asymmetric loss across persona styles (range: 3.11 to 5.37)
  → mixture LoRA partially compensates but doesn't equalize (range: 2.05 to 2.73)
  → the LoRA does more work on hard styles but can't fully close the gap
  → mixture model generates more text in styles it has lower loss on
  → EM assigns higher weight to those personas
  → systematic weight bias (+0.06 for tense_adventure, -0.04 for cheerful_animals)
```

The root cause is the base model's pretrained style distribution. The mixture LoRA compresses a 2.25 nat gap down to a 0.68 nat gap, but the residual asymmetry propagates through generation into EM weight estimates. This is not a failure of the EM method — it's correctly detecting that the mixture model's output distribution is not uniform across persona styles.

### Pretraining as a prior

This has a clean Bayesian interpretation. The base model's pretrained distribution acts as a **prior over styles** — TinyStories contains more literary narration than exclamation-heavy dialogue, so the base model assigns higher probability to those styles. Fine-tuning on uniform persona data is the **likelihood**. The resulting fine-tuned model is the **posterior**, which is a compromise between the prior and the data.

With limited fine-tuning data, the prior dominates — the model's output distribution is pulled toward pretraining preferences. As we increase training data (50 → 300 per persona), the likelihood gets stronger and the posterior moves toward uniform. This is exactly what we observe: the EM weight bias shrinks monotonically with more data.

| Persona | Bias (n=50) | Bias (n=100) | Bias (n=300) | Direction |
|---------|-------------|--------------|--------------|-----------|
| tense_adventure | +0.099 | +0.106 | +0.060 | shrinking |
| warm_family | +0.091 | +0.054 | +0.019 | shrinking |
| cheerful_animals | -0.064 | -0.052 | -0.043 | shrinking |
| blunt_everyday | -0.119 | -0.058 | +0.009 | converging to 0 |

With enough data, the prior would wash out entirely and the EM weights would converge to uniform. The rate of convergence depends on how strong the prior is relative to the data — styles where the base model has strong preferences (like the 2.25 nat gap for `cheerful_animals`) require more data to override.

This reframes what the EM on generations is actually measuring. It is not recovering the training data proportions — it is recovering the model's **effective posterior over styles**. This is the distribution the model will actually generate from, which accounts for both the training data and the base model's inductive biases. For practical model characterization, this is arguably the more useful quantity: you want to know what the model *will do*, not what it was trained on.

The gap between EM-on-training-data (which recovers training proportions) and EM-on-generations (which recovers the effective posterior) is itself a measure of **how much the pretraining prior distorts the fine-tuning signal**. At n=300 this gap is 0.06 - 0.004 ≈ 0.056, indicating that the prior still has meaningful influence at this data scale.

### Recovering the prior directly

To test the Bayesian framing, we generated 200 samples from the **base model** (no LoRA) and scored them with each run's persona models, then ran EM. This recovers the base model's implicit style distribution — the "prior" — as seen through the persona models.

*(Results: `results/data_scaling/base_generations.json`, `results/data_scaling/n*/prior_analysis.json`)*

**Prior weights recovered:**

| Persona | Prior (n=50) | Prior (n=100) | Prior (n=300) |
|---------|-------------|--------------|--------------|
| simple_cheerful_animals | 0.094 | 0.216 | 0.347 |
| literary_melancholic_nature | 0.007 | 0.059 | 0.051 |
| simple_tense_adventure | 0.280 | 0.341 | 0.191 |
| archaic_whimsical_fantasy | 0.276 | 0.102 | 0.158 |
| blunt_factual_everyday | 0.000 | 0.092 | 0.042 |
| warm_nostalgic_family | 0.343 | 0.190 | 0.210 |

The prior is highly non-uniform in all cases. Some personas get near-zero weight (`blunt_everyday` = 0.000 at n=50, `literary_nature` = 0.007 at n=50), while others dominate (`warm_family` up to 0.343).

**Bayesian interpolation test:** If the mixture model's output distribution is a posterior (prior + uniform likelihood), then each mixture weight should fall between the corresponding prior weight and 1/6 (uniform).

| N/persona | Mixture between prior & uniform | Prior-Mixture Spearman | p-value |
|-----------|--------------------------------|------------------------|---------|
| 50 | **5/6** | **+0.886** | **0.019** |
| 100 | 3/6 | +0.829 | 0.042 |
| 300 | 3/6 | +0.143 | 0.787 |

At n=50 (weakest fine-tuning), 5 of 6 mixture weights sit between the prior and uniform — the Bayesian interpolation holds almost perfectly, with significant correlation (Spearman +0.886, p=0.019). The mixture model's output is a clear compromise between what the base model "wants" to generate and what the training data pushes it toward.

At n=300 (strongest fine-tuning), the correlation drops to +0.14 (non-significant). The likelihood has overwhelmed the prior — the mixture weights are close to uniform and no longer correlated with the prior. This is exactly what Bayesian updating predicts: more data → posterior converges to the data, prior washes out.

### Confound: prior depends on persona model quality

The recovered prior weights are not stable across runs. The same base generations scored with different persona models (trained on 50 vs 300 examples) give substantially different prior estimates. `cheerful_animals` ranges from 0.094 (n=50 persona models) to 0.347 (n=300 persona models).

This happens because the prior recovery depends on the persona models' ability to discriminate styles. With n=50 persona models, the pairwise correlation on base generations is 0.999 — the models can barely distinguish styles, so EM is nearly guessing. With n=300 persona models, correlation drops to 0.994, giving EM more signal but still producing different weight estimates.

The "prior" we measure is not a property of the base model alone — it's a joint property of the base model and the persona models used to measure it. The prior and the measurement tool are entangled. This means the Bayesian framing is a useful qualitative model (prior + data → posterior) but the quantitative prior values are instrument-dependent.

Despite this confound, the qualitative pattern is robust across all runs: weak fine-tuning produces outputs that correlate with the prior; strong fine-tuning overwhelms the prior and approaches the training distribution. The direction of the effect is consistent even when the magnitude shifts with persona model quality.

## Red-Teaming & Open Questions

### 1. Non-uniform weight recovery

**The most critical gap.** All experiments used uniform weights. Uniform is the easiest case — even a method that returns "roughly uniform" by default would score well. The real test: train a mixture on [0.5, 0.3, 0.1, 0.05, 0.03, 0.02] and check if EM recovers it. If the method regresses toward uniform when signal is weak (which EM with uniform initialization naturally does), it would look good on our tests but fail on real distributions.

### 2. Unknown or missing personas

We score with the exact persona models used in training. In practice, the persona library may be incomplete. If the model was fine-tuned on a persona not in the library, EM silently distributes that weight across the closest matches — no error signal, no warning. Can we detect "none of the above"? One approach: monitor the EM log-likelihood and compare to a baseline where the data actually comes from a known persona.

### 3. Scaling to larger models

The mixture model's blending ability scales with model capacity. A 35M model produces somewhat incoherent blends; a 7B model could produce fluent text that seamlessly integrates multiple persona styles, making attribution harder. The 0.06 error floor might rise with model scale, not fall.

### 4. Prompt conditioning bias

Generations are seeded with the first 20 tokens of eval examples, which themselves come from specific personas. If those seed tokens carry persona signal, EM partially measures "which persona does the prompt belong to" rather than "what is the mixture model's learned distribution." Test: generate with neutral/empty prompts, or random prompts unrelated to any persona.

### 5. Temperature sensitivity

Temperature 0.8 was chosen without justification. Higher temperature increases stylistic variance (easier attribution, noisier text); lower temperature produces more generic text (harder attribution, higher quality). The results may be sensitive to this. Sweep temperature [0.5, 0.8, 1.0, 1.2] and measure the effect.

### 6. EM local optima

With 6 well-separated personas, EM converges reliably from uniform initialization. With 20+ personas and high correlation, it could get stuck. Test with multiple random restarts and check variance.

### 7. Sample efficiency

200 generated samples with 0.943 correlation gives 0.06 error. Would 1000 samples do meaningfully better, or is the floor set by generation blurriness rather than sample size? Theory suggests $O(1/\sqrt{N})$ improvement if the noise is i.i.d., so 4x samples should halve the error — but if the error is systematic (the blend consistently resembles persona 3 more), more samples won't help.

## Follow-Up Experiments

### Validating the Bayesian prior framing

The qualitative finding — pretraining acts as a prior, fine-tuning as likelihood, output distribution as posterior — is supported but not yet quantitatively robust. The following experiments would harden it:

1. **Prior stability test**: Train two independent sets of persona models on different random subsets of the same data (e.g., two n=300 runs with different seeds). Recover the prior with each set. If the prior weights converge to the same values, the prior is a stable property of the base model. If they diverge, the prior is instrument-dependent and the quantitative framing breaks down. This is the simplest and most important validation.

2. **Scaling persona model quality**: The prior estimates are unstable because persona model correlation on base generations is 0.994-0.999. Train persona models on increasing data (300, 500, 1000, 1400) and track when the recovered prior stabilizes. The threshold where prior weights stop changing is the minimum persona model quality needed for robust quantitative results.

3. **Non-uniform mixtures**: Train mixtures with skewed weights (e.g., [0.4, 0.3, 0.15, 0.08, 0.05, 0.02]) and test whether EM recovers them on both training data and generations. If the Bayesian framing holds, the generation-based EM should recover weights that are "between" the prior and the true skewed distribution. This is the single most important validation — it tests whether the method works beyond the uniform case.

4. **Prior-corrected inference**: If we can measure the prior P(style), we can correct for it. Given the posterior (EM on generations) and the prior (EM on base generations), solve for the likelihood (training proportions) via Bayes' rule: `w_likelihood ∝ w_posterior / w_prior`. Test whether this correction recovers the true uniform weights more accurately than raw EM. If it does, the Bayesian decomposition is quantitatively useful, not just qualitative.

5. **Cross-base-model test**: Repeat the full pipeline with a different base model pretrained on different data (e.g., a GPT-2 variant vs SimpleStories-35M). The prior should change (different pretraining data → different style preferences) but the methodology should still work. If the prior shifts in a way that's consistent with the new base model's known training distribution, that validates the causal claim.

### Other experiments

6. **Sample count sweep**: Fix n=300 training, sweep generation count [100, 200, 500, 1000, 2000] to disentangle sample size effect from LoRA quality effect.

7. **Temperature sweep**: Fix n=300, 500 samples, sweep temperature [0.5, 0.7, 0.9, 1.1] to find the attribution-quality tradeoff.

8. **Prompt conditioning bias**: Generations are seeded with the first 20 tokens of eval examples, which come from specific personas. Test with neutral prompts ("Once upon a time") or unconditional generation to eliminate prompt bias.

9. **Missing persona test**: Train mixture on 6 personas, score with only 4 or 5. Measure how gracefully the method degrades and whether the log-likelihood signals the missing component.

10. **Larger model**: Repeat on SmolLM2-135M or a 350M model to test whether the generation correlation floor rises or falls with scale. The mixture model's blending ability scales with capacity — a larger model could produce more homogeneous outputs that are harder to attribute.

11. **Alternative inference methods**: The EM assumption (data is drawn from a mixture) is wrong for generations. Explore methods that don't make this assumption:
    - Classification-based: for each generation, argmax persona, count frequencies
    - Embedding-space: cluster generations in a learned embedding space, measure cluster proportions
    - Calibrated scoring: normalize persona log-probs against a base model to remove shared variance

12. **Per-token EM**: Instead of one soft assignment per sequence, compute responsibilities per token. This gives ~150x more data points per generation and may capture within-sequence persona variation.

13. **Adversarial personas**: Test with intentionally confusable personas (e.g., two personas that differ only in topic but share all stylistic axes) to find the method's discrimination limit.
